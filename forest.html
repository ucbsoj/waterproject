Are the forests too dense?


University of California scientists say the unnaturally dense forest isn't as water-efficient as it could be.

A dense forest is a thirsty forest...

Due to a century of poor management and fire suppression, the Sierra's vast forests have grown to an unhealthy density. Historically there might have been 100 trees per acre, but now that number is closer to 1,000 per acre.

All those trees need water, and their water comes from the same source as much of California: the mountain snow.

...but if we thin the forest...

Roger Bales, a hydrologist at the University of California Merced, says thinning the trees in the mountains could allow more water to reach the streams.

When the forest is too dense, the snow can't reach the ground and it evaporates. Bales and his colleagues say by removing patches of trees, we can allow more snow to reach the ground and streams.

...that could mean more water for the rest of the state.

Thinning the forest through prescribed burning and mechanical clearing can be controversial. Several sensitive species, including the spotted owl, call the Sierra forests home. Some argue thinning would disrupt wildlife habitat. Prescribed burning also raises air quality concerns.

Bales estimates thinning by 40 percent could yield about 9 percent more water for cities, agriculture and hydroelectricity.
