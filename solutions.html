Leaky Pipes
In the past two years alone, the city of Sacramento has seen almost ten water main breaks. On a particularly bad one in December 2013, over 500,000 gallons of water gushed onto a roadway, stopping traffic for some time. The problem, Metro officials said, was old pipes.

With 240 water main breaks every year and leaking pipes accounting for 7 billion gallons of wasted water daily in the US, spending to replace these pipes may be the best way for a city to reduce water wastage.
In San Diego, where there is an average of 105 water main breaks each year, the city estimates 6.6 million gallons gushed out of ruptured mains in 2013. Last year, the city replaced 28 miles of piping. They are looking to replace all their cast-iron pipes by 2017.
A six-inch water main that broke in San Francisco in Feb 2014 was found to be 81-years old. And just a year earlier, another pipe in the area had burst, also from old age, and flooded 23 homes.

The state Department of Health Services now requires cities to replace at least 10 miles of these old-cast iron pipes each year. Experts say the pipes were not designed to last more than 30 years.
Gray Water and You
In 2010, the California plumbing code approved simple residential gray water systems to be legally installed. Laundry systems without a pump or tank do not even require a building permit.
So, what is gray water, and how will it save you some money?
Gray water systems directly reuse "wastewater" to water your plants. However, to keep your plants alive, only use biodegradable detergents and stay away from soaps, whiteners and bleaches.
According to the Environmental Protection Agency, an average family of four uses 400 gallons of water a day. A gray water system can reuse up to 50% of this water, saving about 70,000 gallons of water each year.
That's water for the family for another half a year.
To ensure safety, gray water should not be used to water fruits and vegetables for consumption. It should be used only via sub-surface irrigation so as to reduce human contact or ponding.
Flushing Better
Each American flushes the toilets in their homes about 5 times per day, according to the American Water Works Association. In a home with regular toilets that use 3.5 gallons per flush, this means about 17.5 gallons per person per day just flushing the toilet.

There are three types of water-efficient toilets:
Single-flush : Uses 1.6 gallons per flush
Dual-flush: Uses 1.6 gallons per flush for solids , 0.8 gallons per flush for liquids Pressure-assist: Uses 1 gallon per flush (tank system compresses air as it fills with water)
Older, water-intensive models of toilets: Up to 7 gallons per flush
Switching to a dual-flush would reduce both water consumption, and bring water savings up by 70%.

Which toilet should you get? Ask about a toilet's gram rating, a standardized laboratory test to determine how many grams of solid material a toilet satisfactorily flushes.
